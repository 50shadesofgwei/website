Maroczy Bind Sicilian

Opening / Middlegame

The opening and middlegame was a standard Sicilian affair at the end of which I achieved a very pleasant position up a pawn, playing for a win.

Endgame

This is the first area where I made an inaccuracy. We will start from the following position, black to move:

The tactical idea is obvious, of course. The king and knight are now on forkable squares, so the next move must be to move the bishop and open up the e3 square, and black can utilise the tempo he gains from white having to defend to create a threat of his own. I however didn’t keep a tight enough hold on the initiative here, and instead played the inaccuracy Bg5?

My idea here was to route the bishop back towards e7, with the idea of exchanging it for the strong d6 knight. I also looked at Bc5 and Bc1, with the idea of attacking the a3 bishop, but these both fail as the rook can take, avoiding the fork and capturing the piece. A move that I clearly did not consider deeply enough was the best move, Bf4!, which forks the now-undefended h2 and already-undefended e5 pawns. e5 is the key pawn here, as it is the lifeline for white’s best piece, the knight on d6. I can only say that this was a blind spot, as it seems a fairly easy move. I remember feeling as though that square was weak somehow, and not calculating any lines starting with Bf4. Perhaps I had tunnel vision on the fork and didn’t spend enough time finding the most precise move.

Poor Conversion

The next error came again in the form of not finding the most accurate continuation, this time starting from the following position:

My plan here was to exchange the knights and win the rook and pawn endgame up a pawn. Even at this point, I had an inkling that the line I went for was drawish. The plan I chose here was to drop the knight backwards to f5, trading it for the white d6 knight. On a purely theoretical level this seems to make sense, trading off white’s strongest piece and being up a pawn in the endgame, but there was still life to be squeezed out of the position here. The strongest move in this position is to drop the knight back to g4, again with a fork on the e5 and h2 pawns. If white now tries to defend his pawn with Rc5, black can chop with …Nxe5, Rxe5 and at the end black has Rxd6, trading the rooks and being up two pawns in a clearly won endgame.
Another move I missed here was the simple Nxg2, also given blessing by the engine. In truth I did not see this, too focused was I on trading the white knight. The intuitive way to find that plan would be to think, ‘I can trade the knights whenever I want. Is there anything else I can do with the knight first?’ I was not in maximum accuracy mode here, clearly.

Terrible Rook Endgame Play

Consider the following position:



Black is +2 here, but there is one move that keeps the evaluation so high, and it is a move I did not even consider. My plan was to target the e4 pawn, first with the rook and then secondly with the king after manoeuvring him closer, and I was dreaming of Rxe5 Rxe5 Kxe5 and being up two pawns in the resulting endgame. This was poorly planned however, as Re8 (the move in the game) allows white to bring his king into the action with Kf3!, preventing any rook capture of e5. Additionally, blacks rook is placed very well on the open D file, allowing him the crushing idea of simply walking his king upwards towards the pawn, being able to block any checks with his own rook. Kf8! is the only move here.

The final blunder (or pair of blunders) in the game came from the following position:

Here the pawn on e5, the pawn that I’ve apparently been so reluctant to capture the whole game, is once again able to be captured… and once again I completely miss it. If this position were a puzzle it’d be rated 300 and I’d solve it in one second. The rook is attacking the f5 pawn (which I didn’t see), the e5 pawn is hanging, AND capturing on e5 would protect the f5 pawn as well. This is the only non-retarded move in the position.
I didn’t see any of that though and instead played Re6.

Now white has the drawing line Rxf5, Kg6, Ke4, protecting the rook and opening up an escape hatch for it to retreat. I was low on time (maybe sub 1 minute), but the moves missed here are unforgivable.
