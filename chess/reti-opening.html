Reti Opening

Opening

Very boring opening affair. He played Nf3 on the first move which already had me somewhat far out of prep. I’ve been playing …d6 as a response online, but in classical a more serious reply might be called for. Just accepting that we’re gonna have a d4 game and playing d5 on move one might be a more practical option.

Nf3 d6 d4 Nf6 g3 g6 Bg2 Bg7 b3 O-O O-O:

Very marginally better for black apparently. The move is e5, because after dxe5 dxe5 there’s all these tactics against the rook on A1. Even if the queens are traded, it still works, although it only works with the follow up Ng4:

White has two pieces hanging here, any other knight move and white can just defend the knight with Bb2.
Instead, not really knowing what to, I just went for the idea I usually go for in the London what with bringing the other knight in via Nbd7, so that’s what I played. Fine, but e5 was better.

Bb2 c5 c4 Rb8 Nc3:

Slight edge for white but nothing major. This position I felt was fine, although somewhat boring and not really sure what the plan is. Here I made an inaccuracy though, and played Qc7?!
What I had missed was the fact that white can just immediately jump into b5 because I have not yet played a6. I’d then be forced to move the queen again into b6 such that I don’t lose the a7 pawn. White can then just play a4 and get this kind of position:

It’s not like black has lost a piece or anything, but there’s no need to allow any of this. White gets a load of space and what are my knight and bishop even doing here? Allowing that knight jump was very lazy from me.

He didn’t go for any of the lines with the knight jump though, and after Qc2 a6 e4? cxd4 Nxd4 we see the position above. I have a clear hole in my game with this kind of position. Here I’m not exactly sure what the thought process was. I saw that the queen only had one defender (the D4 knight), and that likewise the bishop only had one defender (the Queen on C2). I thought b5 cxb5 Nxe4 Nxe4 Qxc2 Nxc2 Bxb2 Rb1 and Bg7 was good for black, and it turns out that it is actually:

But the problem is that white is under no obligation to take with the knight. Qxe4 and Bxe4 are both completely winning for white.
Let’s go back to the key position then:

Why b5? What else was there? I was clearly thinking about tactics and dynamic play, but that seems to be in contrast with the demands of the position. The only other move that I really saw was Ne5, and I thought that was somewhat flimsy, allowing f4 etc. Turns out that Ne4 is a bad move for other reasons as well, black really needs to fight for control of the centre here. If both sides just follow normal development then the position unfolds something like this:

F4 is coming, and the black position is just terribly passive. So what should have been done instead?

E5!, the only move that finds equality for black. I guess conceptually we’re just grabbing a hold of the centre, and concretely we now have a hell of an outpost on d4 if we could land a knight there some day. I don’t think I even saw this move, or perhaps I did and quickly discarded it as it blocks the g7 bishop and creates this Boleslavsky pawn structure with the weakness on d6. What are the benefits to this move then? Well, the d4 knight gets kicked out of its central post and likely goes back to f3 (Nd5 Nxd5 cxd5 Qxc2 Nxc2 Nc5 is also fine for black), it grabs space in the centre, prevents any f4 ideas, removes the pawn weakness on e7 and removes the defender of the Queen. The central control I dare say is the key here. Black simply has no space otherwise. After Nf3, the move recommended by the engine is actually b5.

Its idea is to basically trade down into this position:

Anyhow I didn’t do any of that. I sacked myself into an immediately lost position.

Queen takes! As if I didn’t even consider this move. Bishop takes, also completely winning for white. I remember that on some level I knew this didn’t work, my intuition did not back this idea at all and yet I played it anyway for… some reason? Some perhaps relevant factors here:

  • I was tired.
  • I’d just seen this guy get mated in 10 moves in the previous game.
  • I was impatient, and wanted to play exciting chess.

Are any of these points valid? What can I learn from this?

  • I should prioritise proper sleep during tournaments. Nap if necessary.
  • Assuming this guy would blunder in a line where the forest is neither very deep nor very dark is just a lack of respect. He was 1600 OTB, it’s not like he’s some amateur. I should have given him the proper credit.
  • The proper course of action was to meet the demands of the position. It was not a position ripe for tactics; sure his pieces were somewhat uncoordinated but it’s not like mine were so harmonious either. I’m barely developed and have no space in the centre, of course the tactics won’t work out for me. I suppose I just didn’t want to play some 70 move positional snore-fest where I lose a pawn-down endgame. I’m not sure how to handle these positions and my psychology surrounding them.
