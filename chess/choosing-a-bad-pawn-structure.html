Choosing a Bad Pawn Structure

Starting Position

White has a 3-on-2 on the queenside, and black has a majority in the centre.

The position is technically a draw, but white should be worried about blacks menacing centre. Here the move played in the game by white was Kg2?, but stronger (and the only line to keep the balance for white) was to play b5. In the game I didn’t even consider this move, but it can be found conceptually by thinking that the 3-on-2 queenside majority is white’s best source of counterplay - play on the side where you have a majority and so forth. b5 is not even a sacrifice, indeed it does actually threaten to take BxA BxA Bxa6, winning a pawn and leaving white with two connected queenside passers.

Blacks best move is to take. However, if he tries ignoring the trade and pushing a5 (note here that I didn’t see any continuation here for white, but this is actually now completely winning), white has the winning move b6!:

White here takes control of the c7 square, and will quickly infiltrate with the rook. In this position if black continues with his idea of f5, the game quickly slips through his fingers as after Exf5 Bxf5 white has the amazing move Rc7! counterattacking the d7 rook, which is undefended as the black bishop is pinned to its king.

If, alternatively, black does take on b5, white recaptures with the bishop with a tempo on the d7 rook:

Now black has the unpleasant choice of either moving the rook back to d8 and allowing the c7 infiltration, or moving it to the unseemly e7 square where white follows up with Rd1, attacking the weak d6 pawn.

Instead of any of finding any of this play, white plays Kg2, anticipating the f5-f4 advance.

This concedes the initiative to black, allowing him to play in the centre and make use of his central majority. After the continuation f5 Exf5 Bxf5, this is the position:

We see here already that blacks E and D pawns are going to cause problems for white as they march down the board. This is exacerbated by whites next move: Bxf5+?
After the recapture with the king, blacks king is incredibly active on the 5th rank while whites king has no role in the position. Moving the bishop back to e2 from which it can reposition itself on the long diagonal was a better move.
Nevertheless, play continued Bxf5+ Kxf5 Kf3 e4+ Ke3 d5 Rc5 b6 Rc6 d4+, and white has the choice of moving back to e2 or d2:

In the game, white played Ke2, but this is an inaccuracy. Black will play d3+ at some point and the white king will be forced over to d2 anyway, so e2 here simply wastes a tempo.
Anyhow, play continued Ke2 and black missteps: Rb7?

This takes away the rook from its active role on d7, supporting the passed pawn, and ties it down to the somewhat useless b6 pawn. Whites only choice to equalise the game here is to undermine the black pawn centre with f3!
This idea works because either black takes with ExF Kxf3, after which black no longer has connected passers and the d4 pawn is quite weak, or black advances with e3 and white has the critical resource Rd6!, taking advantage of the fact that black resigned his control of the D file.
