French Defence, Tarrasch Variation

Game began with a French Defence e4 e6 d4 d5 and Nd2, the Tarrasch. Immediately he surprised me by playing c5, which I hadn’t seen before - and I by chance continued according to theory with Ngf3 cxd4 Nxd4 Nf6 e5 Nfd7 N2f3 Nc6.

From here I chose Bb5 and he responded with Qe7 after which I played Qe2, developing the queen and adding some protection to the e5 pawn, but the engine actually prefers castling.

If instead we go down the castling line, there are a number of ways that black can mess up - in fact, all of the captures on e5 fail, some of them quite spectacularly. Ndxe5 runs into Nxe5 Qxe5 Nxc6! and after the pawn recapture there’s a fork of the king and rook on c6. Ncxe5 is met with Nxe5 Qxe5 Re1! and now the queen is in a precarious position:

She must choose her retreat square carefully; the threat is the brilliant Nxe6!! after which black is in some trouble. Qf6 is the only reasonable square and white has to find the counterintuitive idea (and the only move that maintains an advantage) Nf3! , carving out the g5 square for the bishop to develop with tempo and also manoeuvring the knight to jump into e5 where it can add huge pressure on the pinned piece on d7.

Anyhow I didn’t see any of this and instead went down the Qe2 line, after which black responded with a6 Nxc6 bxc6 and Bd3 Nc5 and O-O. Black then took the bishop and I had to make a decision which way was the best way to recapture:

I ultimately went for the queen recapture, I didn’t like that the pawn recapture left me with a backwards pawn that black can undermine with c5, and if I take c5 then black gets a passed pawn in the centre. Qxd3 avoids these lines, and positions the queen on the diagonal with the h7 square, which in conjunction with the bishop and a potential Ng5 jump at some point I thought was much preferable.
The engine has a different evaluation, it recommends taking with the pawn and using it to counteract the black central control that emerges after c5; it’s quite an engine-ish line, very positional to just occupy the centre and slowly watch white run out of moves but it is objectively strongest.
I took with the queen though and he played Be7, Re1 and he castled which was a mistake:

Why is this a mistake? The refutation isn’t trivial. I thought for a long time here, the plan that I wanted was to attack on the kingside as the position just seems so perfect for it, but the lack of a light-squared bishop here makes it very hard to get anything concrete going. As seems to be the case in a lot of my games as of late, I arrived in a position which looked to be the launchpad for an attack, but which bore no concrete plan forward. Prematurely attacking is my most common blunder in these positions, starting the attack and seeking complications too early, while slowly building the pressure and improving the position is better. Here I went with Bg5, I guess hoping that he’d take and I’d take back with the knight and infiltrate with the queen? The stronger idea in this position was actually to play Ng5, threatening the mate immediately and after the bishop captures there is the crazy line h6 Be3 (threatening to jump the bishop into c5 and permanently making black’s bishop terrible), c5 Qc3!

The weakness of the c5 pawn is what gives white the edge in this position. The fork on d4 is useless as it can be captured by the bishop due to the pin on the queen, and c4 looks like it gives black space to work with on the queenside, but is met quite devastatingly with Bd4 (again preventing blacks’ bishop from ever having a meaningful role in the game) and bringing both rooks into the game via e3. This line is +2 for white.
I didn’t see any of that. In fact I remember thinking that after Ng5 Bxg5 Bxg5 h6 Bh4, any chance of an attack on the kingside was gone and the position would just be equal. This was fancy play syndrome on my part, there was no concrete idea with the kingside attack and yet I didn’t look at anything else. There was a hidden line in the position here that would have given me a significant positional advantage but alas I did not see it.

After Bg4 and c5, I was again trying to pull a rabbit out of a hat. h4, doing I’m not exactly sure what.

White immediately returned the favour however and made an inaccuracy of his own, Bb7?
The reason this is an inaccuracy is that it allows the following line: Bxe7 (definitely not a move I had considered) Qxe7 Ng5! and black has to concede a weakness in his kingside pawn structure with g6, lest he get mated.
I didn’t see this though, and continued with my plan of harassing the black kingside with the knight reroute Nh2, to which black responded d4, unleashing the latent potential of the b7 bishop. I brought the knight out to g4 (although stronger was the immediate Qg3, threatening Bh6 and protecting the g2 square) and he pushed me away with h5. Knight back to h2 then, and f6?!

Here I somewhat embarrassingly missed that the e6 pawn is hanging. exf6 Bxf6 Rxe6 regains equality for white. I saw that by dropping the bishop back to f4 I could recapture with the bishop and place it aggressively on the e5 square, hitting the queen and eyeing g7, but this wasn’t the strongest continuation - just taking the pawn is the clear choice here.

Anyway, Bf4 fxe5 Bxf5 Qc6?!

An inaccuracy, but only if immediately punished with Qg6! - I did consider this move, and to be honest cannot quite remember why I didn’t play it. Looking at the engine lines here they both seem to gravitate towards the same evaluation so this likely wasn’t such a big deal.
Play continued Qg3 Rf7 Nf3 Raf8 Re2?

Blunders the fork, which he sees. Preparing this with b3, or playing some waiting move like Rad1 was better. Fact of the matter is that I didn’t even see Qb5 at all. I responded with Rae1 (the only move), and he took the pawn, which is actually inaccurate:

+1.5, says the engine, but the only winning move is Ng5 with the fork of the rook and the e6 pawn. The really nasty idea here is that if black trades the bishop for the knight here, the combination of the pawn arriving on g5 and later g6, plus the rooks infiltrating down the E file, makes the position very dangerous for black now that his queen is offside. For example if he played the move he played in the game (Qxa2) in this position, it’s now +4 for white:

As mentioned before, g6 here is crushing. White here has same the idea that I had later in the game, but here it actually works - if the rook escapes laterally (lets say Re7 for example), how does black prevent ideas of Qg5 Qxh5 Qh7#? If the rook instead moves to f5, white has the brilliant Bxg7!!

The follow-up here is important. After Kxg7 (technically an inaccuracy but what else are you gonna play here), the key move is Rxe6! - White can win back the bishop with Qc7+, but that concedes the biggest strength of white’s position, the fact that the black king is in a mating net.
If after Rxe6 Qd5 Re7+ Kh6, Rh7 is mate. Even with great defence, the passed pawn on the 6th rank and the weakness of blacks king is sufficient compensation for the piece.

Back to the game though, because this wasn’t what I played.

Qg6 was my choice, forking the pawns on e6 and h5. The best move here for black was to simply capture the f3 knight and mess up white’s kingside pawn structure; I had thought that maybe this was fine for white as it would allow me to build up pressure down the G file and eye the g7 square again, but in practice this might not work. Before looking at it with the engine I see that it’s gonna take quite a few moves to organise the white pieces towards g7 (Kh2, Rg1, Rg2 Re1 Rg1 is a long plan), and in the meantime black can capture the hanging h4 pawn and create a passer on the kingside. My king is in bad shape in that line, and its not clear where my pressure against the king is gonna come from.

Black passes the ball back into the white court with the next move however, Qxa2 - defending the e6 pawn, but allowing the Ng5 jump again. After Bxg5 hxg5 Qd5, this is the position:

The engine really wants f4, says its the best move by far. It does make sense once you look at it a bit deeper, again the key strength of white’s position is the monster pawn on g5, and f4 supports that pawn and is in turn supported by the bishop, who now gains another defender. That Be5 f4 g5 structure is suffocating for black, what are the black rooks doing now? Plus (as if the position wasn’t good enough for white already), f4 prevents black from ever having any play against the g2 square with the battery on the a8-h1 diagonal, as the rook now defends laterally. I’m not sure why I didn’t consider this move, I can only think it’s another case of the premature attack - wanting to blow open the floodgates and win spectacularly when the more accurate play is to slowly build the advantage and prevent counterplay, such that when the attack does start there are no counter-shots available for the opponent. f4 clearly best here, despite it (temporarily) blocking the rooklift.

Re4 was my choice, succumbing to fancy play syndrome as per the norm. Why specifically is this weaker? Well, first of all it’s a self pin, the rook can’t move lest white gets mated on g2, and second of all, as we’ll see shortly, the fact that the key g6 pawn is now only defended by the queen will turn out to be a major liability in the position. The idea of course is to shift the rook over to h4 and go for mate on the h7 square, an idea that I was willing to sacrifice an exchange for, but the engine doubts the validity of the idea. Either way, it blunders equality as opposed to any loss of material, but still a very instructive moment.

Black takes his foot off the gas pedal with Qd7, revealing the attack of the bishop on the e4 rook, and I misstep by capturing the h5 pawn. The only move that holds the position is the one he played, taking the offered exchange with Bxe4. Now Rxe4 which I had seen and his follow-up Rf5, which I had not. As soon as he played this I got the sense that perhaps I had lost the thread of the position, and the engine seems to agree as it says the only option that isn’t just comfortably better for black is to go for some crazy line with g4 Rxf2 g6 Rf1+ Kh2 R8f2+ but that looks disastrous for white practically speaking.

He plays Qe7 here which is a slight inaccuracy, and again here the engine really wants f4 (f4=1.44 for black, next best move is Bd6 with 2.66 for black). I honestly don’t know why I didn’t look at f4 here, it makes so much sense - I guess because I was so dead set on the rook sliding over to the H file to threaten that mate that I didn’t concentrate enough on preventing his counterplay. With my next move, I was dead lost:

Now Qxe5 and Rg4, and black is completely winning, but I’ll admit that I didn’t see how. Neither did my opponent though (at this point I will mention that he was horrifically low on time, maybe 20 seconds), not surprising though as the way to convert this win is actually really clever:

Qc1+, which I saw, Kh2 Rh5+ Rh4 Rxh4+ Qxh4 and the winning idea: Qh6!

First forcing the king onto the H file, then trading rooks and forcing the queen onto the H file as well, then dropping the queen back and creating the pin that forces the trade of queens. If he found this over the board even with enough time on the clock I’d of been pretty impressed, super clever idea. He didn’t see this though, and instead finally succumbed to the pressure and played the losing move: Qe7??

Now mate is forced: Rxg7+ Qxg7 Gxg7# ends the game. What a rollercoaster!
