Which Way to Sac the Knight? (Morozevich vs Sadvakasov, 2001)

We start in the following position, white to move, black having just played Rfe8. It is clear that the position is full of latent dynamism; the weak pawn on f7, the air around the black king, the d4 knight ready to jump into the black kingside and the bishop and rook in good attacking position.

White needs to take advantage of his superior position by opening up the black king - but how? White gets nowhere after Qxf7+ Kxf7 Rf1+ Kg8 Ne6 Qd7 Bxg6 Rf8, and any capture with the bishop critically weakens the c2 square. By process of elimination therefore we arrive at the knight moves. We can intuit these moves somewhat easily, but the continuation after the sacrifice has to be very precise. The first move that came to mind for me was Ne6!!, which is easier to spot as it comes with the threat on the queen, but the stronger move in the position was actually Nf5!!

But what happens if they don’t take? It’s still +3.5 for white but it’s not immediately clear how. First of all we have to spot the defensive resource Bf8!, the only move that puts up any serious resistance.

Why does Bf8 Work?

Simple enough once you ask the question; it defends the dark squares around the king and opens a lateral defence from the queen onto the f7 square. To understand why this move is good, let’s look at why the other options don’t work. Nxd5 threatens the queen but in appearances only - Nh6+ king somewhere Qxf7 and black is dead.
