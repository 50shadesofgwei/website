Grinding out a Winning Position

Starting Position

This is a position from the Chessly Endgames course, in the practical rook endgames section. White is up a pawn, but there is no immediate win; the pawn on c4 is hanging so Nxc4 is possible, but that allows blacks knight to jump into e2 with check, winning the c3 pawn right back. The only continuation here is Kf2.

In the study however, white immediately captures on c4. After the follow up Ne2+ Kf2 Nxc3 and the knight drops back to e3 (note that Ne5 was stronger due to the more aggressive placing of the knight and the fact that the rook maintains eyes on the weak e4 pawn), and black attacks white’s a2 weakness with Be6.

The position is equal here, but white has some nice ideas that he needs to find in order to maintain equality. The key idea is that despite white not being able to defend his A pawn, after the trades his rook can slide over to a1 and skewer the pawn on a7. Thats not the end of the story though as white has to contend with his weak e4 pawn, so after the variation Rc1 Nxa2 Bxa2 Bxa2 Ra1 and black slides his bishop out of harms way to b3:

White can capture the a7 pawn, but then black simply captures e4 right back and the game is a dead draw - white has a nice idea here though, which one can find via the following train of thought:
Firstly, the rook is attacking the E pawn, and moving the E pawn simply loses a tempo and allows black to protect his pawn by playing Re7. Is there any other way to prevent black capturing it then? If we follow the checks, captures and attacks guideline then we should see the nice zwischenzug Ra3!, taking advantage of the fact that black has no good retreat squares for the bishop. D1, c2, c4 and d5 are all controlled by the knight, while a2 and a4 are controlled by the rook. Here black has no choice but to retreat the bishop to e6 and block the rooks line of sight with the pawn.
