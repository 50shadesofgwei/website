Polish Opening

Opening

Opening was a Polish, to which I responded d5. The game opened with b4 d5 Bb2 Nd7 e3 e5 b5?, resulting in the following position:

Black is better here. Not for any tactical reasons, but he has easier, quicker development and better control of the centre. The best move here is c5, because if white doesn’t take then black simply has a huge centre, and if white does take then black still ends up with a huge centre after BxC. Anyhow, I continued with the less accurate but also fine …Bd6 c4 Ngf6 Cxd5 Nxd5 Nc3, where this is the position:

This is where i started to give away the advantage. The only move here to keep the edge for black is to take the knight with …Nxc3. I did not consider this move in the game, instead I thought that by bringing the other knight out I can recapture with that and free up my c8 bishop. The reason that this is inaccurate, as i was soon to find out, is that with the knight undefended on d5 it can be harassed by the white bishop, developing with tempo.

The best continuation here is simply to take the knight on c3, and after Bxc3 just to castle. In this line we reach the following position:

Black has no problems here and is ahead in development.

Instead of this, I brought out my other knight to defend, and after Nf3, we reach this position:

Whites last move isn’t the best choice according to the engine, but it does have a threat. I didn’t see any kind of threat here, and simply castled. I had missed the fact that the e5 pawn, although seeming well protected by the d6 bishop, is actually threatened. Even looking at this position now, knowing the tactic, the position looks so boring that my tactic alarm still isn’t being triggered. Nevertheless:

Immediately taking advantage of my oversight. Here, after taking back with the knight, there is nothing black can do about Nxe5 and he is down a key central pawn. The game of course continued Nxd5 Nxe5, and this is where the tilt really took hold: f5??

An unforgivably terrible move. My half-baked idea here was that since white is not yet castled and black is, and black has these d6 and c8 bishops pointed menacingly at whites kingside when he does finally get castled, black can balance the loss of his kings’ pawn with active kingside play. Instead, this hands a huge positional advantage and the initiative soundly over to white, who now immediately refutes my terrible idea with the best move: Bc4!

This is a miserable position for black, who must now bid farewell to any ideas of a kingside attack and scramble to defend his weakened diagonal. I continued with Bxe5 with a discovered defence of the bishop, then after Bxe5 I slid the king out of the pin with Kh8.

What should black have done instead?

The mature continuation is simply to accept that the pawn is lost and to continue developing normally, and hope to generate some counterplay later in the game. I was not good enough to realise this.
