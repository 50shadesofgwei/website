The Cow

Summary

Need to be more accurate in judging the activity of the enemy pieces in their attack. Just because a position looks ugly, doesn’t mean it can’t be defended.

Position 1

My judgement here was that after dxc5 Bxc3 bxc3 Qa5, I thought that looked too much like mate. The reality of the situation is that black’s threats can be parried, partly because white can just move out of the way of the checks, and partly because white gets a lot of counterplay with cxd6!

The only move is to take the pawn. Importantly, the check on b4 is covered, so black’s only check is the one on a3, and after white slides the king over to c1, there’s no forcing follow up, and black just has to deal with the passed pawn on the D file.

Position 2

I did think this was an inaccuracy (Nc6 seemed stronger), but I definitely did not consider my position as a +2-level advantage. Let’s see why the engine thinks this is so good for white. The key move: f5!. Also, the only winning move. I did have my eye on this pawn break as being the foot in the door for opening up the black kingside, but I didn’t think I had time to play it here for some reason. The key detail that I missed here was that b4 actually does give me the time to start executing my own plans, black can no longer follow up with Nc6 Nb4 because his own pawn is blocking it. f5 must be answered by black because if not, f6 is just a clear fork. I did not accurately evaluate that right now, in this exact position, white must play to start his own counterplay, as opposed to securing the defence, which for now is fine due to the b4 inaccuracy.

I played e5 with the idea of blunting the bishop on g7, but this was somewhat unnecessary. The diagonal is blocked for the foreseeable what with the pawn on d4 being defended 3 times, which again, gives white time to start his own attack. White needs some lines to be opened if he hopes of ever having any counterplay here. I just overestimated black’s attack here, and underestimated my own. Giving black the d5 square was also a significant error, as now the knight can jump right into the centre and start pressuring my king.

Position 3

Here I played Rd3 with the idea of preventing any of this Na3+ nonsense (subtle foreshadowing), but a better idea, again given +2 by the engine, is to just take the pawn on d6. I didn’t seriously consider this move because the reason I played e5 in the first place was to block the g7 bishop, and capturing the pawn would undermine that idea. What I didn’t consider, but is clearer to me now, is that capturing on d6 is a blunder because black just continues with his plan of sticking his rook on b8 and sacking the knight on a3. If black takes back on d6, then the position is +1 for white, but black is under no obligation to do so.

What’s actually better is to capture with dxc5!, and after the queen captures back, only now play Ne4, as it comes with tempo on the queen
