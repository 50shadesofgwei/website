Sicilian Defence - Dragon Variation

A good example of why one ought to play on in losing positions when there’s still play on the board. A careless error on my part threw the game, but I kept posing practical problems and for my efforts my opponent returned the favour 10 moves later and threw the game right back, which was kind of him.

Opening

e4 c5 Nf3 d6 d4 cxd4 Nxd4 Nf6 Nc3 g6 Be3 Bg7 f3 O-O Qd2 Nc6 O-O-O d5:

And now I’m out of prep. I’m not really sure what to do against this d5 break, it’s not the kind of position that I’m looking for on the white side of a dragon. Best according to the engine is to take the D pawn, after which Nxd5 Nxc6 (only move, gives black a pawn weakness) Bd4 (again only move, counteracting the strong g7 bishop) e5 Bc5 (again the only move) Re8 Ne4 and this is comfortably better for white. Back has a weakness on c6 and the g7 bishop isn’t so much participating in the game. h4 and g4 are the moves here for white, and the plan is still to launch everything at the black king.

Anyhow, I took the knight on c6, which it turns out is a book move. Bxc6 obviously and then Bh6 from me, looking to trade off his dragon bishop. To my surprise, he took of his own accord - Bxh6 Qxh6:

And Qb6 as shown above. There’s only one move in this position which doesn’t blunder away any advantage for white, and it was not the one I played. Best here is the immediate h4, looking again to strum up some kingside attack - the engine always seems to favour these ideas in the dragon, even when queens are off and there is no big closed centre flank attack. Anyhow, I took on d5 with exd5 and he played the zwischenzug Rb8, after which b3 Nxd5? Nxd5 and cxd5:

Now the engine reads this as better for white, but not with the move I played. The most natural move in the position is to capture the pawn right? Of course, but here again h4 is better. I didn’t even consider this move to be honest, it wasn’t on my radar, but even if I did see it I can’t imagine I’d of played it. There’s a free pawn to take there! plus, do we really want black to get e6 in? Turns out we’re not really so bothered about the pawn or about e6. Black can’t play e6 right away because then what is the bishop doing, and in the time it takes to bring out the bishop and probably trade it off on d3, white has sufficient time to play h4-h5 and get this typical anti-dragon pawn play going:

This position, despite being equal material, is +1 for white. The line is a long one, but the key ideas are that white has this battery against h7 which black needs to be very careful about, and in an endgame black has an isolated pawn on the A file.
Another key idea (and a really smart one!) is that blacks seemingly dominant kingside/central pawn structure is actually undermined quite severely from this simple h5 idea - black must take with the F pawn and open the lateral defence of the h7 square, else all of white’s mating ideas start to work, so the solidity of this black pawn chain is in appearances only. Clever!

All that was lost on me though; monkey see pawn, monkey take pawn: Rxd5?

My justification for this was that I had the follow-up move Rb5 with the tempo on the queen, but alas I stopped calculating there. This was fine, although he had a nice continuation with:
Be6 Rb5??
