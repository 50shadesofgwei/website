Scandinavian Defence, Leonhardt Gambit

Opening - Hang on to the B Pawn!

After the b4 gambit, black slides the queen back to b6, which is an inaccuracy. The best way to play for white against this move is to play Rb1, protecting the pawn and also lining up the rook against the queen. Note here the importance of protecting the pawn, after white declines the gambit we have to change our conception of the position from ‘sacrificed a pawn for development’ to ‘just play chess’. Black’s setup in these lines is notoriously resistant to complications and tactical shots, and so white must suck it up and accept that the ensuing game will be a positional struggle.

Yes, white gains some tempi if the queen were to capture, but of what good are they? Look at the resulting position after black drops the queen back to d6:

Black is behind in development, but there are no open lines, no weak squares, no foot in the door nor guiding thread for any kind of sacrificial attack. Black, as in all these lines in the Scandinavian, is utterly solid. So there’s no payoff for gambiting the pawn! This is why Be2 is an inaccuracy - it’s also why the later Be3 is an inaccuracy; I should value pawns a tad higher in these positions.
